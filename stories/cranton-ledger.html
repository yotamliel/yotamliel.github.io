Roman Mana enters Cranton mayoral race as council weighs short-term rental cap after surge in nuisance complaints

The proposal would cap permits, raise fines, and require faster response to complaints. Mana says the city needs “an enforcement-first reset” that protects neighborhoods without punishing responsible hosts.

CRANTON, CA
City leaders are weighing new rules for short-term rentals, including a permit cap and higher penalties for verified nuisance violations.

CRANTON, Calif. — Roman Mana, a former county IT manager, filed Thursday to run for mayor in Cranton’s upcoming special election, turning the city’s escalating fight over short-term rentals into the first major flashpoint of the campaign.

Mana, 50, announced his candidacy outside the Municipal Services Center and called for what he described as “a balanced reset” on short-term rental rules—tightening enforcement against problem properties while preserving a legal pathway for residents who rent out spare rooms or their homes part-time. “People shouldn’t have to live next to a revolving door party house,” Mana said. “But we also shouldn’t punish responsible hosts because the city hasn’t enforced its own rules.”

The filing comes days after the City Council advanced a draft ordinance that would cap the number of short-term rental (STR) permits citywide and steepen penalties for violations. The proposal cleared the council’s Public Health and Neighborhoods Committee in a 4–1 vote Tuesday and is expected to reach the full council for a vote in early February, according to the committee agenda.

A proposed cap, tougher fines, and faster response

The draft ordinance would limit STR permits to 1% of Cranton’s housing units, create district-level caps to prevent clustering, and require a “local contact” able to respond to complaints within 30 minutes. It would also raise the city’s civil fine for verified nuisance violations from $250 to $1,000 and authorize license suspension after three substantiated incidents within a 12-month period.

City staff cited a sharp rise in complaints: 311 records show 486 STR-related calls in 2025, up from 214 the year before, driven primarily by noise, parking, and trash complaints in the Riverbend and North Cranton neighborhoods, according to a staff memo presented Tuesday.

The incident that intensified the debate

The fight accelerated after a weekend gathering at a rental on Waverly Street drew multiple complaints and a late-night police response, according to residents who spoke at Tuesday’s meeting. Neighbors described guests blocking driveways and leaving broken bottles on the sidewalk. One resident, Devon Riley, said the property had hosted “at least five large groups since October.”

Police spokesperson Lt. Andrea Kline said officers responded to the location just after 12:30 a.m. on Jan. 11 and issued a citation for a noise ordinance violation. Kline declined to comment further, citing an open administrative review.

Hosts push back: “Don’t regulate us out of existence”

Local hosts and property owners say the proposal goes too far and could wipe out legitimate rentals that support household budgets. “The city is acting like every host is a bad actor,” said Kara Dole, who said she rents out a basement unit on weekends to help pay medical bills. Dole argued that a hard cap could freeze permits even for residents with strong compliance histories.

“Enforce the rules you have, and target repeat offenders rather than restricting supply across the board.”

Renters and neighborhood leaders: “It’s a housing issue now”

Renters and neighborhood groups argue the issue is no longer just about nuisance properties. They say investor-run STRs are removing long-term units from the market and pushing rents higher.

“Every house that becomes a weekend rental is one less home for a family,” said Councilmember Janine Patel, who sponsored the ordinance and represents parts of North Cranton. Patel said her office has received more than 300 emails on the issue since November.

Mana echoed that framing but stopped short of endorsing the council’s cap as written. Instead, he proposed an “owner-occupied first” approach—allowing STRs more freely when the host lives on-site while requiring stricter limits on investor-operated whole-home rentals. “If you live there, you have skin in the game,” Mana said.

Where the race goes from here

Cranton voters will decide the next mayor in April after former Mayor Lynn Hartwell resigned last month for health reasons, according to the city clerk’s office. The acting mayor will continue to serve until the election.

Mana said he will release a full housing platform next week, including proposals for rental inspection staffing, a public dashboard of licensed STRs, and a mediation process for neighbors and hosts. Pollack has not said whether she will seek a full term, and at least one sitting council member is “seriously considering” entering the race, according to two people familiar with local discussions.

For now, the fight over who gets to live where—and under what rules—is already shaping the tone of the campaign. “This isn’t about being anti-tourist,” Mana said. “It’s about protecting neighborhoods and making sure housing is for residents first.”
